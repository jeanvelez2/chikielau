Review: The Midnight Library

By Chikielau • January 20, 2026 • 5 min read

The Midnight Library

by Matt Haig

★★★★★ 5/5 stars

Genre: Contemporary Fiction, Fantasy

Published: 2020

Pages: 304

Get This Book

A Journey Through Infinite Possibilities

What if you could undo your regrets? What if you could try every different version of your life? Matt Haig's The Midnight Library explores these profound questions through the story of Nora Seed, a woman who finds herself in a magical library between life and death, where each book represents a different life she could have lived.

This isn't just a fantasy novel—it's a deeply philosophical meditation on choice, regret, and the meaning of a life well-lived. Haig has crafted something truly special here, a book that will resonate with anyone who has ever wondered "what if?"

The Premise

Nora Seed is at her lowest point. She's lost her job, her cat has died, and she feels like a disappointment to everyone around her. In a moment of despair, she finds herself in the Midnight Library, a place between life and death where she can sample different versions of her life—the life where she became an Olympic swimmer, the life where she married her ex-fiancé, the life where she became a glaciologist.

Each book in the library represents a different choice, a different path. And Nora has the opportunity to live them all, to find the "perfect" life that will make her want to stay alive.

What I Loved

The concept is brilliant, but what makes this book truly special is Haig's execution. He doesn't just use the premise as a gimmick—he explores it deeply, thoughtfully, and with genuine emotional resonance. Each life Nora tries teaches her something new about herself, about what she values, and about what truly matters.

Haig's writing is accessible yet profound. He tackles heavy themes—depression, suicide, existential crisis—with sensitivity and hope. The book never feels preachy or heavy-handed, even when it's exploring deep philosophical territory. Instead, it feels like a conversation with a wise friend who understands your struggles.

"Between life and death there is a library, and within that library, the shelves go on forever. Every book provides a chance to try another life you could have lived. To see how things would be if you had made other choices... Would you have done anything different, if you had the chance to undo your regrets?"

The supporting characters, particularly Mrs. Elm (the librarian), are wonderfully drawn. Mrs. Elm serves as both guide and conscience, helping Nora navigate her journey without dictating her choices. Their relationship is one of the book's emotional anchors.

The Emotional Impact

I won't lie—this book made me cry. Multiple times. But they were good tears, cathartic tears. Haig has a gift for capturing the small, beautiful moments that make life worth living. A perfect cup of tea. A conversation with a friend. The feeling of swimming in the ocean. These moments accumulate throughout the book, building to a powerful emotional crescendo.

What struck me most was how the book reframes the concept of a "perfect" life. Through Nora's journey, we come to understand that perfection isn't about making all the "right" choices—it's about accepting the life you have, with all its messiness and imperfection, and finding meaning in it anyway.

A Few Minor Quibbles

If I had to find fault, I'd say that some of the alternate lives feel a bit rushed. We don't spend enough time in some of them to fully appreciate their nuances. Additionally, the ending, while emotionally satisfying, wraps things up perhaps a bit too neatly. But these are minor complaints in what is otherwise a remarkable book.

Who Should Read This?

This book is for anyone who has ever felt stuck, anyone who has ever wondered if they made the wrong choices, anyone who has ever struggled to find meaning in their life. It's for people who love philosophical fiction but don't want to wade through dense academic prose. It's for readers who want to be moved, challenged, and ultimately uplifted.

If you've enjoyed books like A Man Called Ove, Eleanor Oliphant Is Completely Fine, or The Humans (also by Matt Haig), you'll love this. It has that same blend of humor, heart, and profound insight into the human condition.

Final Thoughts

The Midnight Library is a book that will stay with you long after you turn the final page. It's a reminder that every life has value, that it's never too late to change, and that sometimes the life you have is exactly the one you need. In a world that often feels overwhelming and hopeless, this book is a beacon of light.

Matt Haig has written something truly special here—a book that is both entertaining and profound, accessible and deep. It's a book that will make you think, make you feel, and ultimately make you grateful for the life you're living, with all its beautiful imperfections.

My Rating

★★★★★ 5/5

Verdict: A must-read for anyone seeking hope, meaning, and a reminder that life is worth living.
